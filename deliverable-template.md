# Deliverable template

The one-page write-up the client receives within 48 hours of the call. Fill in everything in square brackets.

The deliverable is one page. Roughly 600-800 words. Long enough to be substantive, short enough to be read once and acted on.

---

```markdown
# AI Workflow Audit: [Client Name]

Prepared by [Your Name] - [Date]
Audit call: [Date and time]
Attendees: [Client attendees] · [You]
```

## What goes in each section

A few notes on the sections that are easy to get wrong.

### "What it replaces"

Don't write "saves time." Write what specifically goes away. "Removes the 4-hour Friday report-pulling session" is useful. "Saves admin time" is not.

### "Confidence rating"

If the rubric Confidence was 3 or below, the rating in the deliverable is Medium or Low. Match the rubric. Don't inflate to make the recommendation more attractive.

### "Why this is #1"

Two sentences max. The Why isn't the Impact (you already said that). It's the relationship between Impact, Effort, and Confidence. Example: "Highest Impact in the shortlist with Effort low enough that you can ship it in your next two working sessions and Confidence high enough that your existing team can own it without external help."

### "Where to start"

Pick the highest-Confidence, lowest-Effort workflow for the opening shot. Not the highest-Impact. The team needs an early win more than they need the biggest possible payoff. If the team ships their first workflow and uses it for a month, the next two get built. If the team's first workflow is too ambitious and stalls, none of them get built.

### "What NOT to build"

This is the section clients reference most. It's the section that proves you're not just selling them work. Pick the workflow the client was most excited about that scored badly on the rubric. Explain why in two sentences.

Example: "Avoid building the website chatbot. Confidence is too low - no one on your team has owned a live customer-facing agent before, and the failure modes (mis-bookings, wrong information, escalation gaps) are exactly the kind that erode trust faster than the chatbot saves time. Revisit in 6 months once you've shipped the three workflows above."

### "Open questions"

Two purposes. First, they're the inputs you need before scoping a paid build engagement. Second, they're a courteous way of surfacing things the client should resolve internally that you noticed during the call.
